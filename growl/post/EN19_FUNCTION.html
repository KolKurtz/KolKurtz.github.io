A MUSICAL FUNCTION PRIMER

Function is an immense subject. Not only does it underpin a lot of composition and appreciation of music, but it also asks questions about what role we have as musicians. This article is intended as a jumping off point for further study because really your role will determine what in function matters to you. I'd warn NEVER to use my less technical articles (like this one) as a source. My definitions for things often don't agree with the established ones. That doesn't stop me thinking mine are right! At least they are for me.

A LOOSE DESCRIPTION

Function is, VERY loosely, how and why music works. I mean music in the scale of whole pieces down to complete phrases. Function differs from tonality because the latter determines what notes belong in a scale, how those chords are formed and what melody notes accord with them. Tonality I'd say, works at the phrase level and below. So maybe we better discuss tonality...

WHAT IS TONALITY!?

Tonality is the whole raft of expectations, idioms, "rules" and practices that form an identifiable system of music. The most obvious example of such a system is Western tonality (as in the Western world). Music in the west grew up out of the major and minor scale. By virtue of those we know what notes, chords and thus progressions we are likely to hear in music. Further, because we are so used to hearing them, we have widely come to prefer certain patterns within that system. Not without reason are most pop songs written around the 1, 4 and 5 chord of the major scale - they are fulfilling expectations.

Tonalities are not set however. Western music in the last century has evolved, and although niche, traditional laws and tastes are highly eroded in some areas. Suffice it to say though, hundreds of years of repeated practices have established a working system.

By way of example; our western tonality tends to suggest that a C (CEGB) major chord played in accompaniment is unlikely to underpin a completely chromatic (all the tones C,C#,D,D#...) melody. It is yet more unlikely to go with a melody in the key of C# major (C#,D#,E#,F#,G#,A#,B#). Those melody notes don't belong in the C major scale. Tonality in the form of our expectations AND our practices means there are things we do and don't do. These aren't rules, they are just things we've gotten used to.

HOW DOES FUNCTION MAKE MUSIC WORK?

To re-enforce what we expect from a tonality, function acts to clarify things like key and structure. The main way it accomplishes this is by establishing harmony through consonance and dissonance and indicating which note is tonic (home).

Consonance and dissonance exist as a continuum. They sit as extremes at either end of a sliding scale. Not only will musicians argue from a theory standpoint about what is dissonant, but the everyday listener will do it too. When you hear live musicians emit a bum note you just experienced a dissonance. That note did NOT fit with the underlying chords and bass and someone is about to get fired. That's an extreme example, we even find dissonance WITHIN musical scales.

Try playing a major 7 chord, for example Cmajor7 (CEGB). It is a nice nostalgic chord, right? Mostly, yes, but play just the C and the B alone. That is NOT so nice. If you play the B one note lower than the C, it is even worse. How can this be? Surely we're in key, all the notes are "right". What you are experiencing is a dissonant interval within they key. The worst of all, an absolute howler, is the diminished 5th. Every major scale spells out a dominant 7th chord from its 5th note. In C major, for example, this is G7 (eg: GFBD). If you play that chord, it is bluesy, almost jovial. However, if you play just the 3 and 7 of the chord (as B-F or F-B), woah! Dissonance produces tension.

Tension can be reduced in music by "resolution". Just as we build tensions by adding dissonant intervals, by following those intervals with consonant intervals that tension is reduced. Most often this is accomplished by taking the tones making up a dissonant interval and either separating them or moving them closer together so that they are no longer dissonant. In our G7 example above, we could resolve that diminished 5th interval by following the G7chord (GFBD) with (CEGC) Cmajor. F-B is now E-G and we have replaced the diminished 5th with a far mor consonant minor third. This is a gross simplification of something called "voice leading", keeping track of the component voices inside harmonic blocks like chords. Again, it is a vast subject and a cornerstone of composition.

Something else has happened in our example of G7 to CMajor. If you play the two chords in sequence you can't help notice the feeling of arriving home. Whilst the dissonance resolving has something to do with it, the movement of G to C in the lowest notes of the chord is VERY pleasing. In western tonal harmony, the descent of a fifth is the strongest bass movement possible. This is an example of a musical "cadence". Powerful, complete sounding changes in harmony like this serve as the punctuation points in music. They can be used to end phrases, re-iterate where "home" is and even inform the listener that we are at a new "home". In our G7 to CM example we would actually say that the G7 chord "functioned" as a dominant. As composers we have deliberately used a chord that states the end of a musical unit by returning home.

HARNESSING THE TENSION

Whilst it is possible to write a song where the goal is to minimize dissonance as much as possible, I would argue that it isn't much of a song. Without a doubt, a person's preferences are a question of taste, but arguably we ALL like drama in our music. At least, we enjoy some form of contrast and emotional storytelling. The deliberate use of consonance and dissonance in composition creates moments of tension and release. As listeners, we experience that usually as emotion. Music activates memory, provokes feelings and fuels thought.

As you can see, we've entered the role now of music critic! This has happened because composers usually (I hope) write with intent. Critics feel (rightly or not) in a position where they can evaluate how well music presses their buttons. A large part of this is due to how well the music is functioning.

IN CONCLUSION

I hope this isn't too wooly an article to serve as over-view. The idea here is to show how function pervades the writing, playing and appreciation of music. By thinking in terms of structure and the action of harmony we're able to infer some rules and patterns for the effects of those structures.
